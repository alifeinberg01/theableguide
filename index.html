From the classroom to the road

Strategies that actually work

The same evidence-based tools I use in my special ed classroom — adapted for airports, hotels, and theme parks.

01

Prime before every transition

Tell your child what's coming before it happens. "In 5 minutes, we're going to put on our shoes." Give a 5-minute, 2-minute, and 1-minute warning whenever possible.

02

Use first-then language

"First we go through security, then we get a snack." This simple pattern reduces anxiety by showing your child there's something good on the other side.

03

Offer controlled choices

Instead of open-ended questions, give 2 clear options: "Do you want to sit by the window or the aisle?" Choice reduces power struggles.

04

Reinforce specifically & immediately

"You walked through the security scanner — that was so brave!" Specific, immediate praise teaches your child exactly what they did right.

05

Create a sensory break plan

Know in advance where quiet spaces are. Set a schedule — every 90 minutes, find a quiet spot for 10-15 minutes. Be proactive, not reactive.

06

Have a de-escalation kit ready

When you see early signs of distress, intervene early. Pull out headphones, offer a chewy, find somewhere quieter. Prevention is far easier than recovery.

Our Story

I built the resource
I wish existed.

I am a moderate/severe special education teacher. I have spent years in classrooms building visual schedules, writing behavior plans, running IEP meetings, and helping families figure out how to navigate a world that wasn't designed with their child in mind.

And every time a parent mentioned wanting to take a trip — Disney, a beach vacation, flying to visit family — I watched the same thing happen. Their face would fall a little. Not because they didn't want to go. Because they had no idea how.

The travel blogs weren't written for them. The family-friendly hotel reviews didn't mention noise levels or whether there was a quiet room. Nobody was writing about how to actually prepare a child with autism for an airport.

I kept thinking: I know how to do this. I know what a visual schedule looks like. I know how to write a social story. I know what sensory breaks need to look like in an unpredictable environment. Why isn't anyone putting this together?

So I did. WanderKind is the resource I wished I could hand every parent who sat across from me at an IEP table and said, quietly, "We haven't been on a vacation in years."

You deserve to travel. Your child deserves to be in the world. I'm here to help make that possible.

— Alexandra Feinberg, Founder

What to say
Scripts for every situation
At the ticket gate
"My child has a disability that makes waiting in long lines very difficult. We have a DAS pass. Can you help us get started?"
Cast Members are trained for this. They will take you to a separate window to activate your DAS pass for the day.
Requesting a return time
"We would like a return time for [ride name], please. We have a DAS pass."
Show your DAS on the app or your physical card. You receive a time to come back — like a restaurant reservation. You can hold one return time at a time.
If something unexpected changes
"My child has autism and wasn't expecting this change. Can you help me explain what happened and what we can do instead?"
Cast Members often offer a compensation return time or help finding an alternative. Don't hesitate to ask.
If your child needs to leave immediately
"We need to find a quiet space right away. Can you point us toward First Aid or the nearest calm area?"
Any Cast Member can direct you. Don't wait for a meltdown to escalate before asking for help.
Transition warning sequence
"In 30 minutes, we are going to start walking to the front gate."
Follow with 15-minute, 5-minute, and 1-minute warnings. Then: "It's time! We did it." Always end by asking your child for their favorite moment.
When things go sideways
Backup plans for hard moments
If your child refuses to ride anything
  • 1
    Do not push or negotiate. Acknowledge: "You don't want to ride right now. That's okay."
  • 2
    Walk through themed areas — Galaxy's Edge and New Orleans Square are visually stunning without any rides.
  • 3
    Offer a sensory-friendly alternative: "Do you want to get a snack and watch people walk by?"
  • 4
    Let your child lead. Some of the most meaningful moments happen outside of rides.
If a meltdown begins
  • 1
    Get to a quiet spot immediately — do not try to talk while walking or in a crowd.
  • 2
    Do not give instructions or ask questions. Wait it out in silence.
  • 3
    Offer headphones and a comfort item. Provide sensory input if your child seeks it.
  • 4
    Once calm, offer water and a snack. Wait 10 more minutes before any activity.
  • 5
    Decide together: stay or go. A partial success is still a success.
If the day is going badly from the start
  • 1
    If significant distress continues past the first 90 minutes, trust your gut.
  • 2
    Leave. Return to the hotel or car. This is not failure — it is wisdom.
  • 3
    Celebrate what you did do: "We made it to the park! We rode Pirates. That was brave."
  • 4
    You can return another day. You cannot undo a traumatic experience.
If you get separated
  • 1
    Before entering, write your cell number on your child's arm in marker, or give them a card to carry.
  • 2
    Teach your child: "If you can't find me, find someone with a name tag and stay with them."
  • 3
    Any Cast Member will take your child to Guest Relations at City Hall immediately.
  • 4
    Go to the nearest Cast Member if your child is missing — do not search alone.

Disability access passes

Every theme park access
pass — explained

Disney, Universal, Legoland, SeaWorld — every major park has a different program with different names and requirements. Here's exactly how each one works.

🏰

Disneyland & Walt Disney World

Disability Access Service (DAS)

Most comprehensive

What it does

DAS allows guests who cannot wait in a conventional queue due to a disability to receive a return time equal to the current wait. You wait anywhere in the park — not in line — and return when it is your turn.

How to get it

  • Pre-arrival (recommended): Video chat with a Disney cast member through the My Disney Experience app or at disneyland.com/das. Available 2–30 days before your visit.
  • Day of: Visit Guest Relations at the park entrance. Bring documentation if available, though Disney does not require a specific diagnosis.
  • You must describe the specific nature of the disability and why waiting in a conventional queue is not possible.

Key details

  • One return time can be held at a time
  • Covers the guest with the disability + up to 3 additional family members (or up to party size for smaller parties)
  • Valid for the length of your visit (up to 120 days)
  • Pre-registration via video chat is strongly recommended — day-of lines at Guest Relations can be long
Alexandra's tip: Do the video chat pre-registration. It takes about 10 minutes and means you walk straight into the park on travel day without any extra steps at Guest Relations.
🎬

Universal Studios (Hollywood & Orlando)

Attraction Assistance Pass (AAP)

Requires IBCCES card

What it does

The AAP allows guests to wait in a more comfortable location instead of the standard queue and receive a return time based on the current wait.

How to get it

  • Step 1 (required): Register for an IBCCES Individual Accessibility Card (IAC) at ibcces.org at least 48 hours before your visit. Free to apply. May require documentation.
  • Step 2: On the day of your visit, bring your IBCCES card to Guest Services at the park entrance to receive the AAP.

Key details

  • IBCCES registration is required — you cannot get the AAP without it
  • Process the IBCCES card well in advance (ideally 2+ weeks before your visit)
  • Covers the guest + up to 3 additional guests
Alexandra's tip: The IBCCES requirement catches a lot of families off guard. Do this at least 2 weeks before your trip — not 48 hours before.
🧱

Legoland California & Florida

Disability Access Pass (DAP)

Most beginner-friendly

What it does

The DAP allows guests with disabilities to avoid waiting in the standard queue. Legoland is widely considered one of the most accommodating parks for guests with special needs — staff tend to be very patient and proactive.

How to get it

  • Visit Guest Services at the front of the park on the day of your visit
  • No pre-registration required
  • Bring documentation if available — it can speed up the process

Key details

  • No IBCCES card required (unlike Universal)
  • Covers the guest + up to 5 additional family members
  • Ride attendants are trained and generally very supportive
Alexandra's tip: Legoland is genuinely excellent for sensory kids — lower stimulation than Disney, smaller crowds, and staff who really get it. If this is your family's first theme park, consider starting here.
🌊

SeaWorld & Busch Gardens

Ride Accessibility Program (RAP)

Requires IBCCES card

What it does

The RAP provides return times for attractions so guests do not need to wait in the standard queue.

How to get it

  • Register for an IBCCES IAC card at ibcces.org before your visit (same as Universal — one card works for both)
  • Bring your IBCCES card to Guest Services at the park on the day of your visit
Alexandra's tip: If your family is planning to visit both Universal and SeaWorld on the same trip, your IBCCES card works at both — you only need to register once.

Quick reference

Park Pass Name Pre-register? IBCCES needed? Guests covered
Disney (DL / WDW) DAS Yes (recommended) No Guest + up to 3
Universal Studios AAP Required (IBCCES) Yes Guest + up to 3
Legoland DAP No No Guest + up to 5
SeaWorld / Busch RAP Required (IBCCES) Yes Varies
